Course Overview

Everything you need to know about this course and the IELTS General Training exam.

What is IELTS General Training?

IELTS General Training is designed for those migrating to English-speaking countries (particularly Canada, Australia, UK) or for work/training purposes. The test measures your ability to communicate in English across all four language skills: Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking.

Test Duration

2 hours 45 minutes total — Listening (30 min), Reading (60 min), Writing (60 min), Speaking (11–14 min on a separate day).

Scoring

Each skill scored on Band 1–9. Your Overall Band Score is the average of all four. CLB 9 requires L:8.0, R:7.0, W:7.0, S:7.0.

Month 1: Foundation

Build core skills. Learn test formats, timing, and basic strategies. Daily vocabulary building and grammar review.

Month 2: Intensive

Deep-dive into each skill. Timed practice tests. Targeted work on weak areas. Advanced strategies and techniques.

Month 3: Mastery

Full mock tests under exam conditions. Fine-tune timing. Perfect weak areas. Build exam-day confidence and stamina.

Listening — Target: Band 8.0+

30 minutes + 10 minutes transfer time · 40 questions · 4 sections

Test Format

Section 1: A conversation in an everyday social context (e.g., booking a hotel, asking for information). Easiest section.

Section 2: A monologue in a social context (e.g., a tour guide describing a location, a radio announcement).

Section 3: A conversation between 2–4 people in an educational/training context (e.g., university tutorial discussion).

Section 4: A monologue on an academic subject (e.g., a university lecture). Hardest section — no break in the middle.

Question types: Multiple choice, matching, plan/map/diagram labelling, form/note/table/flow-chart/summary completion, sentence completion.

Strategies for Band 8+

1. Read Ahead

Use every second of reading time to preview questions. Underline keywords. Predict answer types (name? number? place?). This is the single most important strategy.

2. Listen for Signpost Words

Words like "however," "actually," "but," "in fact" signal a correction or important point. The answer often comes RIGHT AFTER these words.

3. Watch for Traps

Speakers often change their mind: "I thought 3 PM... actually, let's make it 4." The answer is always the FINAL decision. Don't write the first thing you hear.

4. Spelling Counts

Misspelled answers = zero marks. Practice common tricky words: accommodation, schedule, receipt, Wednesday, February. Keep a spelling list.

5. Never Leave Blank

No penalty for wrong answers. Always guess if you don't know. Even a wild guess has a chance. Check the word limit (e.g., "no more than two words").

6. Practice at Higher Speed

Listen to podcasts/videos at 1.25x speed during practice. When you return to normal IELTS speed, it feels slower and you catch more detail.

Step-by-Step Approach During the Test

1As soon as you're told to open the booklet, immediately read the questions for Section 1. Underline key words.
2Predict what kind of answer you need (a name, a number, a date, a place). Write this prediction lightly in pencil.
3Listen carefully. Write answers as you hear them. Don't get stuck — if you miss one, move on immediately to the next question.
4During the 30-second breaks between sections, quickly read ahead to the next section's questions.
5In the 10-minute transfer time at the end, carefully copy answers to the answer sheet. Check spelling, capitalization, and word limits.

Reading — Target: Band 7.0+

60 minutes · 40 questions · 3 sections

Test Format (General Training)

Section 1 (Easiest): 2–3 short texts about everyday topics — advertisements, timetables, notices, instructions. ~14 questions. Target time: 15 minutes.

Section 2 (Medium): 2 workplace-related texts — job descriptions, company policies, training materials, contracts. ~13 questions. Target time: 20 minutes.

Section 3 (Hardest): 1 long, complex text on a general interest topic — similar to a magazine article or book excerpt. ~13 questions. Target time: 25 minutes.

Question types: Multiple choice, identifying information (T/F/NG), identifying writer's views (Y/N/NG), matching information, matching headings, matching features, matching sentence endings, sentence/summary/note/table/flow-chart/diagram completion, short-answer questions.

Strategies for Band 7+

1. Time Management Is Everything

Section 1: 15 min. Section 2: 20 min. Section 3: 25 min. Set a watch/timer. If you spend too long on one question, circle it and move on. Return later if time permits.

2. Read Questions FIRST

Never read the whole passage first. Read the questions, underline keywords, then scan the text for those keywords. This saves enormous time.

3. Paraphrase Detection

IELTS uses synonyms and paraphrases. "Costly" in the question might be "expensive" in the text. Build a strong awareness of common paraphrases.

4. T/F/NG vs Y/N/NG

TRUE/FALSE = factual information in the text. YES/NO = the writer's opinion/claim. NOT GIVEN = the text simply doesn't mention it — don't infer or assume.

5. Matching Headings

Read the first and last sentence of each paragraph. These usually contain the main idea. Eliminate easy matches first, then work on ambiguous ones.

6. Never Leave Blanks

No negative marking. Guess strategically. For T/F/NG, if you're stuck, "NOT GIVEN" is a safe statistical guess as many students under-use it.

Step-by-Step Reading Approach

1Glance at the passage title and any headings/images to get the topic (10 seconds).
2Read ALL the questions for that section. Underline key words in each question.
3Scan the text for answers to the first set of questions. Answers usually follow the order of the text.
4For T/F/NG: Find the relevant sentence, then compare it EXACTLY to the statement. If the text says something different = FALSE. If the text doesn't address it at all = NOT GIVEN.
5Transfer answers as you go (no separate transfer time for Reading). Double-check spelling and word limits.

Writing — Target: Band 7.0+

60 minutes · 2 tasks · Task 1 (letter, 150+ words) + Task 2 (essay, 250+ words)

Test Format (General Training)

Task 1 (20 min, 150+ words): Write a letter — formal, semi-formal, or informal. You're given a situation and 3 bullet points to address. Letter types include: complaint, request, apology, invitation, recommendation, application.

Task 2 (40 min, 250+ words): Write an essay. Types include: agree/disagree, discuss both views, problem/solution, advantages/disadvantages, two-part question. Task 2 is worth TWICE the marks of Task 1.

Scoring criteria: Task Achievement, Coherence & Cohesion, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy (each 25%).

Strategies for Band 7+

1. Address ALL Bullet Points

Task 1: Cover every bullet point fully. Missing even one drops your Task Achievement score significantly. Allocate roughly equal space to each.

2. Plan Before You Write

Spend 3–5 minutes planning. For Task 2: write your thesis, 2 main points, and examples. A planned essay is always better than an unplanned one.

3. Use Correct Tone

Formal: "Dear Sir/Madam" → "Yours faithfully." Semi-formal: "Dear Mr. Smith" → "Yours sincerely." Informal: "Dear John" → "Best wishes." Getting this wrong costs marks.

4. Paragraph Structure

Each body paragraph: Topic sentence → Explain → Example → Link back. This structure ensures coherence and makes your argument easy to follow.

5. Vocabulary Range

Don't repeat the same words. Use synonyms: important → crucial, significant, vital. Show range but stay natural — don't force complex words you're unsure about.

6. Mix Sentence Structures

Use a mix of simple, compound, and complex sentences. Include: conditionals, relative clauses, passive voice, and reported speech. Aim for 1 complex sentence per paragraph minimum.

Sample Band 7+ Answers

Prompt: You recently bought a product online that arrived damaged. Write a letter to the company. In your letter: describe what you ordered, explain the problem, and say what you want them to do about it.

Dear Sir or Madam,


I am writing to express my dissatisfaction with a recent purchase I made through your online store on 15th March. I ordered a ceramic dinner set, model "Elegance Collection," comprising twelve plates, six bowls, and six cups, for which I paid $89.99.


Unfortunately, when the package arrived on 22nd March, I discovered that three of the plates were cracked and one of the cups had a chip on its rim. It appeared that the items had been inadequately packed, as there was minimal protective material inside the box. This was extremely disappointing, particularly as the set was intended as a gift for my sister's housewarming party.


I would appreciate it if you could either send replacement items for the damaged pieces or provide a full refund for the entire set. Additionally, I believe it would be beneficial for your company to review its packaging procedures to prevent similar issues in the future. I have retained the damaged items and the original packaging as evidence, should you require them.


I look forward to your prompt response on this matter.


Yours faithfully,
Mubashir Ahmed

Why this scores Band 7+: Clear purpose from the opening. All 3 bullet points covered in full. Appropriate formal register throughout. Good range of vocabulary (dissatisfaction, inadequately, beneficial). Mix of sentence structures. Coherent paragraphing. Correct opening/closing conventions. 185 words.

Prompt: Some people believe that technology has made our lives more complicated rather than simpler. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

It is often argued that modern technology, rather than simplifying our daily routines, has introduced a new layer of complexity into our lives. While I acknowledge that technology can occasionally be overwhelming, I largely disagree with this view, as the benefits it provides far outweigh its drawbacks.


On the one hand, it is understandable why some individuals feel that technology complicates life. The constant stream of notifications from smartphones, the need to learn new software at work, and the pressure to maintain a social media presence can all contribute to feelings of stress and information overload. For instance, many office workers report spending a significant portion of their day managing emails rather than completing productive tasks, which can lead to frustration and reduced efficiency.


However, I believe that the advantages of technology overwhelmingly simplify our existence. Firstly, routine tasks that previously consumed hours — such as banking, grocery shopping, and booking travel — can now be accomplished within minutes through mobile applications. Secondly, communication technology has made it possible to maintain relationships across vast distances at virtually no cost, something that would have been inconceivable just a few decades ago. Furthermore, access to information has been democratised; anyone with an internet connection can learn almost any skill through free online resources, from cooking to coding.


In conclusion, although technology undeniably introduces certain challenges, its capacity to streamline everyday activities and connect people globally makes our lives considerably simpler on balance. The key lies not in rejecting technology, but in learning to use it judiciously.

Why this scores Band 7+: Clear position stated in intro and maintained throughout. Both sides discussed (balanced approach). Specific examples given. Strong vocabulary range (democratised, inconceivable, judiciously). Complex sentences with relative clauses and conditionals. Clear 4-paragraph structure. Effective use of cohesive devices. 278 words.

Speaking — Target: Band 7.0+

11–14 minutes · 3 parts · Face-to-face with examiner

Test Format

Part 1 — Introduction & Interview (4–5 min): General questions about yourself, your home, work/studies, hobbies, daily routine. Give 2–4 sentence answers. Be natural and friendly.

Part 2 — Individual Long Turn (3–4 min): You receive a cue card with a topic and bullet points. You get 1 minute to prepare and must speak for 1–2 minutes. The examiner may ask 1–2 follow-up questions.

Part 3 — Two-way Discussion (4–5 min): Abstract questions connected to the Part 2 topic. The examiner wants to see analytical thinking, opinion expression, and the ability to discuss complex ideas.

Scoring criteria: Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, Pronunciation (each 25%).

Strategies for Band 7+

1. Extend Your Answers

Never give one-word answers. Use the formula: Answer + Reason + Example. "Do you like cooking?" → "Yes, I do. I find it quite relaxing, especially after a long day. For instance, I often make pasta from scratch on weekends."

2. Part 2: Use Your Prep Time

In the 1-minute prep time, write KEYWORDS only (not full sentences). Jot down: 2–3 key points, a specific example, and relevant vocabulary. This structure will carry you through 2 minutes easily.

3. Self-Correct Naturally

If you make a grammar mistake, correct it naturally: "I goed... I mean, I went to the cinema." This actually helps your score — it shows grammatical awareness. Don't panic about mistakes.

4. Use Fillers Wisely

Natural fillers are fine: "That's an interesting question...", "Let me think about that...", "Well, I'd say that..." They give you thinking time and sound natural. Avoid "um" and "uh" excessively.

5. Show Vocabulary Range

Use idiomatic expressions naturally: "It's right up my alley," "That rings a bell," "To be honest." Use less common vocabulary: "rewarding" instead of "good," "hectic" instead of "busy."

6. Pronunciation Matters

Focus on clarity, not accent. Practice word stress (PHOtograph vs phoTOGrapher), sentence stress (emphasize content words), and intonation (voice goes up for questions).

Sample Band 7+ Answers

Part 1 Example: "Do you prefer reading books or watching films?"

"That's a tough one, actually. I'd have to say I lean more towards watching films, primarily because I find them more immersive — you know, the visual effects and the soundtrack really draw you in. Having said that, I do enjoy reading before bed. There's something about getting lost in a good novel that's incredibly relaxing. But if I had to choose one, films would win, especially sci-fi ones."

Analysis: Extended answer with reasons. Natural fillers ("That's a tough one"). Hedging language ("I'd have to say"). Contrast ("Having said that"). Specific detail ("sci-fi ones"). Fluent delivery.

Part 2 Cue Card: "Describe a skill you learned as a child"

"I'd like to talk about swimming, which I learned when I was around seven years old. My father taught me at a local pool near our home. Initially, I was terrified of the water — I distinctly remember clinging to the edge of the pool and refusing to let go. But my father was incredibly patient. He started with floating exercises and gradually built my confidence over several weeks. The breakthrough moment came when I finally swam across the width of the pool without any support. I was over the moon! Since then, swimming has become one of my favourite activities. I try to swim at least twice a week, and it's become not just exercise but a form of meditation for me. Looking back, I'm grateful my father persisted because it taught me that facing your fears often leads to the most rewarding experiences."

Analysis: Clear structure (what, when, who, how, feelings). Specific details and emotions. Idiomatic language ("over the moon," "clinging to the edge"). Past and present tenses used accurately. Good vocabulary range. Speaking time: approximately 1.5–2 minutes.

Tips, Strategies & Common Mistakes

Expert advice to avoid costly errors and maximize your band score on exam day.

⚠️ Common Mistakes That Cost Marks

Listening: Not reading ahead

Most students try to read and listen simultaneously. Use preview time to read questions BEFORE the audio starts. Predict answers.

Listening: Spelling errors

"Accomodation" instead of "accommodation," "Febuary" instead of "February." These count as wrong. Keep a spelling list.

Reading: Running out of time

Most common mistake. Don't spend 25 min on Section 1. Stick to: 15-20-25 minute splits. If stuck on a question for 2+ minutes, skip it.

Reading: Confusing False & Not Given

FALSE = the text clearly contradicts the statement. NOT GIVEN = the text doesn't address it. If you can't find it in the text, it's NG.

Writing: Not answering all bullet points

Task 1 gives you 3 bullet points. Missing one = Band 5 max for Task Achievement. Read the prompt twice. Check your letter covers all 3.

Writing: Under word count

Task 1: minimum 150 words. Task 2: minimum 250 words. Under word count = automatic score reduction. Always aim for 160+ and 270+.

Speaking: Memorized answers

Examiners are trained to spot memorized responses. They sound unnatural. Instead, prepare IDEAS and VOCABULARY for topics, not scripts.

Speaking: One-word answers

"Do you like music?" → "Yes." ← This scores Band 4. Always extend: answer + reason + example/detail. Aim for 3–4 sentences minimum.

💡 Top 10 Exam Day Strategies

1Sleep 8 hours the night before. Cognitive performance drops 25% with poor sleep. No late-night cramming.
2Eat a proper breakfast. Your brain needs fuel. Avoid heavy/sugary food. Oatmeal, eggs, and fruit are ideal.
3Arrive 30 minutes early. Last-minute rush = stress = lower performance. Settle in, relax, breathe.
4Read instructions carefully. "No more than TWO words" means "three words" is wrong even if correct. Follow the rules precisely.
5Never leave any answer blank. No negative marking in IELTS. A guess is better than empty. You have nothing to lose.
6Writing: Start with Task 2. It's worth double the marks. Give it your best energy and time (40 min). Then do Task 1 (20 min).
7Speaking: Be natural and friendly. Treat it like a conversation, not an interrogation. Smile, make eye contact, be yourself.
8Manage your time ruthlessly. Wear a watch. Check it every 10 minutes. If you're behind, speed up. Don't perfect one answer at the expense of others.
9Stay calm if you get a hard question. Not every question is meant to be easy. Do your best, move on, and recover on the next one.
10Trust your preparation. You've spent 12 weeks preparing. You know the format, the strategies, the traps. You are ready.

Final Exam Day Breakdown

Listening (30 min)

Preview questions aggressively. Write answers as you hear them. Don't get stuck — keep moving. Use the 10 min transfer time to check spelling. Aim: 35+/40.

Reading (60 min)

15 min Section 1, 20 min Section 2, 25 min Section 3. Read questions first. Scan for keywords. Never read the whole passage first. Aim: 30+/40.

Writing (60 min)

Start with Task 2 (40 min). Plan for 5 min, write for 30 min, review for 5 min. Then Task 1 (20 min). Check word count. Proofread if time allows.

Speaking (11–14 min)

Be natural, extend answers, use examples. In Part 2, use all 1 min prep time. Don't memorize — prepare ideas and vocabulary. Self-correct gracefully.
